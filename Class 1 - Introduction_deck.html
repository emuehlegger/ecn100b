II. Profit maximization (Perloff 8.2)

Firms seek to maximize profits

  • We assume that the firm’s objective is to maximize profits, subject to a variable the firm controls (often quantity).

maxqΠ(q)=TR(q)−TC(q) \max_{q} \Pi\left(q\right)=TR\left(q\right)-TC(q)

  • Taking the derivative and setting equal to zero.

dΠ(q)dq=dTR(q)dq−dTC(q)dq=MR(q)−MC(q)=0 \frac{d\Pi\left(q\right)}{dq}=\frac{dTR\left(q\right)}{dq}-\frac{dTC\left(q\right)}{dq}=MR\left(q\right)-MC\left(q\right)=0

  • Rearranging, we get the condition for firm profit maximization

MR(q)=MC(q) MR\left(q\right)=MC\left(q\right)

II. Perfect competition in the short run (Perloff 8.3)

What are the assumptions of perfect competition?

  1. There are many, many firms
  2. Firms sell identical products
  3. All firms have full information about price and product characteristics
  4. There are no transaction costs
  5. Entry and exit from the market is free

These assumptions have two important implications.

  1. Firms are price takers. They can sell as much as they want without affecting the market price.
    • This means that firms face a flat marginal revenue curve →MR=P\rightarrow MR=P.

  2. Free entry and exit will occur if there are economic profits or losses.
    • In the long run, economic profits are driven to zero.

Short-run competitive equilibrium

  • The competitive equilibrium is the price and quantity where market supply equals market demand.
  • We can find the competitive equilibrium in three steps:
    1. Calculate the firm supply curve, how much each firm will produce as a function of price.
    2. Sum the firm supply curves horizontally to get the market supply curve, how much all firms will produce as a function of price
    3. Equate market supply and market demand.

We’ll consider a specific example

  • In this example, we assume the following:
    • Market demand is QD=600−50PQ_{D}=600 -50P
    • The market is perfectly competitive
    • There are 100 identical firms in the market
    • Each firm has identical costs: TC(q)=9+q2TC(q) = 9 + q^2

  • Question: What can we learn about firm costs just from looking at the equation for TC?

Step 1: What is the firm supply curve?

  • To find the firm supply curve, maximize profits wrt to q.

maxqΠ(q)=TR(q)−TC(q)=P*q−(9+q2) \max_{q} \Pi\left(q\right)=TR\left(q\right)-TC\left(q\right)=P*q -\left(9+q^{2}\right)

dΠdq=P−2q=0→P=2q→q*=12P \frac{d\Pi}{dq}=P -2q=0\rightarrow P=2q\rightarrow q^{*}=\frac{1}{2}P

  • There are two things to note here.
    1. The firm is maximizing profits, because MR = MC.
    2. Since the market is perfectly competitive, MR = P.

III. Perfect competition in the long-run (Perloff 8.4)

In our example, do the firms earn economic profits?

  • Each firm produced q=3q=3 and sold at P=6P = 6.

Π=P*q−(9+q2)=18−(9+32)=0\Pi =P*q -\left(9+q^{2}\right)=18-\left(9+3^{2}\right)=0

Economic profits

  • In the example above, firms earn zero economic profits.
  • What does this mean?
    • Economic profits include the opportunity cost of capital.
    • If a firm earns zero economic profits, their return in this industry is as good as the return they could receive from an equally risky industry.
    • Consequently, firms do not want to enter nor exit the market.

Can firms ever earn economic profits?

  • In the short run, firms can change quantities, but can’t enter or exit the market.
    • Thus, they can make short-run economic profits or losses.

  • But, in a competitive market in the long run, firms can freely enter and exit the market.
    • Firms will enter if there are economic profits and exit if there are economic losses.
    • Entry (exit) will shift the supply curve out (in) until profits are zero.

Example with an inward demand shock

  • Consider restaurants at the start of COVID, earning zero profits.
    1. COVID shifts demand inward.
    2. Price falls, firms bear economic losses in the short run.
    3. Firms exit, supply shifts inward until economic profits are zero.

IV. Perfect competition and welfare

Social Welfare and Perfect Competition

  • Consumer surplus is the amount, in dollars, that consumers benefit from the existence of the market.
    • Graphically, CS is the area below the inverse demand curve, but above price.

  • Producer surplus is the amount, in dollars, that producers benefit from the existence of the market.
    • Graphically, PS is the area above the inverse supply curve, but below price.

Market Failures and Welfare

  • The perfectly competitive market maximizes welfare.
  • But, there are three common sources of market failure, times when the unregulated market may not maximize welfare.
    1. Firms are not price-takers (e.g., monopoly)
    2. Actions create benefits or impose costs on others (e.g., air pollution)
    3. Some parties have information that others don’t (e.g., used car reliability)
  • This is where economics brings real value to policy and outlines this course!

Major takeaways

  • Perfectly competitive firms set MR = MC to maximize profits.
  • Price-taking means that MR = P. A firm can sell as much as it wants at price P without affecting the market price.
  • Horizontally adding the firm supply curves together gives the market supply curve.
  • In the short-run, firms in a perfectly competitive market can earn economic profits or bear economic losses. But, in the long-run, free entry and exit will drive economic profits to zero.
  • The perfectly competitive market maximizes welfare and doesn’t create deadweight loss.
  • But, if firms are not price takers, actions create externalities, or information is asymmetric, deadweight loss might exist in the unregulated market.
